\begin{abstract}
Maxwell's Demon is traditionally resolved through information-theoretic arguments: the demon must pay an entropy cost to measure, store, or erase information about molecular velocities. We present a more fundamental resolution: the demon commits a category error. Entropy counts spatial arrangements ($\Omega$), not velocities. Since $\partial \Omega / \partial v = 0$, sorting by velocity has zero effect on entropy. The demon manipulates a kinetic property (velocity) while the Second Law protects a configurational property (entropy). These belong to orthogonal ontological categories---manipulating one cannot affect the other. This resolution requires no information-theoretic arguments: even with infinite memory and zero erasure cost, the demon fails because velocity-sorting is categorically orthogonal to entropy. We prove three central results: (1) the velocity independence of arrangement count, (2) the snapshot principle showing identical configurations exist at any temperature, and (3) elastic collision entropy invariance demonstrating that temperature can change without entropy change. Experimental validation confirms heat-entropy decoupling: heat fluctuates bidirectionally while entropy increases monotonically through categorical completion. The demon's strategy fails not due to measurement costs but due to attacking the wrong quantity entirely.

\textbf{Keywords:} Maxwell's Demon, entropy, velocity-entropy independence, category error, Second Law, Boltzmann entropy
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%==============================================================================

In 1867, Maxwell introduced a thought experiment that has challenged thermodynamic foundations for over 150 years \citep{maxwell1871theory}. A being with ``faculties so sharpened that he can follow every molecule in its course'' observes molecules in two chambers at thermal equilibrium, separated by a door. By selectively opening the door for fast molecules moving one direction and slow molecules moving the other, the demon appears to create a temperature difference without work, violating the Second Law.

\subsection{Historical Context}

The demon has inspired over a century of analysis. Key developments include:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Szilard (1929)}: Connected measurement to entropy, showing that acquiring information about a molecule's position costs at least $\kB T \ln 2$ \citep{szilard1929entropieverminderung}
\item \textbf{Brillouin (1951)}: Argued that the demon must use light to observe molecules, and this light carries entropy \citep{brillouin1951maxwells}
\item \textbf{Landauer (1961)}: Proved that erasing one bit of information dissipates at least $\kB T \ln 2$ of heat \citep{landauer1961irreversibility}
\item \textbf{Bennett (1982)}: Showed that measurement can be thermodynamically reversible; the entropy cost lies in erasure \citep{bennett1982thermodynamics}
\end{itemize}

The dominant resolution, developed by Szilard, Brillouin, Landauer, and Bennett, locates entropy costs in information processing. Measurement requires interaction that generates entropy. Memory erasure dissipates at least $\kB T \ln 2$ per bit. These arguments are logically consistent but share a limitation: they accept the demon's strategy as sound in principle and locate failure in implementation costs.

\subsection{A More Fundamental Resolution}

We propose a more fundamental resolution: \textit{the demon's strategy is unsound in principle}. The demon commits a category error by treating velocity (a kinetic property) as if it determined entropy (a configurational property). These quantities belong to orthogonal ontological categories. Manipulating one cannot affect the other.

This resolution is stronger than the information-theoretic resolution:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Assumption} & \textbf{Information} & \textbf{Category Error} \\
\midrule
Demon strategy correct? & Yes (in principle) & No \\
Free measurement? & Demon succeeds & Demon fails \\
Free erasure? & Demon succeeds & Demon fails \\
Zero-cost implementation? & Demon succeeds & Demon fails \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Even granting the demon all information-theoretic resources---infinite memory, zero measurement cost, zero erasure cost---the demon still fails because \textit{sorting by velocity cannot change entropy}.

\subsection{The Core Insight}

The Boltzmann entropy is:
\begin{equation}
S = \kB \ln \Omega
\label{eq:boltzmann}
\end{equation}
where $\Omega$ is the number of accessible microstates. The crucial question is: what does $\Omega$ count?

$\Omega$ counts \textit{spatial arrangements}---the number of ways molecules can be positioned in the available volume. It does not count velocity distributions. A microstate in the canonical ensemble (fixed temperature) specifies positions $\{\mathbf{r}_i\}$; momenta are integrated out according to the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

This immediately implies:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial v_i} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{\partial S}{\partial v_i} = 0
\label{eq:independence}
\end{equation}

Entropy is independent of velocity. Sorting by velocity cannot change entropy. The demon's entire strategy operates on a quantity (velocity) that is orthogonal to the quantity protected by the Second Law (entropy).

\subsection{Category Error}

A category error occurs when one treats a property of one ontological type as if it were a property of another type. The demon assumes:
\begin{equation}
\text{Sort by velocity} \Rightarrow \text{Change entropy}
\end{equation}

This assumes velocity and entropy are in the same category. They are not:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Kinetic properties}: $\mathbf{v}$, $E_{\text{kin}}$, $T$, $\mathbf{p}$---derivatives of position with respect to time
\item \textbf{Configurational properties}: $\mathbf{r}$, $\Omega$, $S$---functions of position alone
\end{itemize}

These categories are independent. Specifying kinetic properties does not determine configurational properties. The demon's strategy is analogous to trying to change an object's color by changing its weight---the properties are in different categories with no causal connection.

\section{What Entropy Actually Counts}
\label{sec:entropy_counts}
%==============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Boltzmann Entropy]
\label{def:boltzmann}
The Boltzmann entropy of a macrostate is:
\begin{equation}
S = \kB \ln \Omega
\end{equation}
where $\Omega$ is the number of microstates compatible with the macrostate. The macrostate specifies macroscopic observables ($V$, $N$, $E$); microstates specify complete microscopic configurations.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Microstate]
\label{def:microstate}
In the spatial interpretation relevant to entropy counting, a microstate is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Microstate} = \{\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N\}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{r}_i$ is the position of molecule $i$. In the canonical ensemble, entropy counts spatial configurations with momenta integrated out.
\end{definition}

The number of microstates $\Omega$ depends on:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Volume $V$: larger volumes allow more positions
\item Number of molecules $N$: more molecules create more arrangements
\item Excluded volume: molecules cannot overlap
\item Potential energy constraints: molecules avoid high-energy configurations
\end{enumerate}

\textit{None of these factors depend on velocity.} Volume is geometric. Excluded volume depends on molecular size, not speed. Potential energy depends on intermolecular distances $|\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j|$, not velocities.

\begin{theorem}[Velocity Independence of Arrangement Count]
\label{thm:velocity_arrangement}
The number of spatial arrangements $\Omega$ is independent of molecular velocities:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial v_i} = 0 \quad \forall i
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Spatial arrangements are determined by positions $\{\mathbf{r}_i\}$. Velocity is the time derivative: $\mathbf{v}_i = d\mathbf{r}_i/dt$. Velocity determines the rate of change of position, not position itself.

At any instant $t$, positions $\{\mathbf{r}_i(t)\}$ determine the spatial arrangement. Velocities $\{\mathbf{v}_i(t)\}$ determine how arrangements will evolve, but not the current arrangement. Two systems with identical positions but different velocities have the same arrangement count $\Omega$.

Therefore $\Omega = \Omega(\{\mathbf{r}_i\}, V, N, \text{interactions})$ with no dependence on $\{v_i\}$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial v_i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial v_i}\Omega(\{\mathbf{r}_j\}) = 0 \qquad \qed
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Velocity Independence of Entropy]
\label{cor:entropy_independent}
Entropy is independent of molecular velocities:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial S}{\partial v_i} = \frac{\kB}{\Omega}\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial v_i} = 0
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}
This independence may seem surprising given traditional thermodynamics' emphasis on temperature. The resolution: entropy depends on configuration count (spatial), while temperature depends on velocity distribution (kinetic). A system can have high kinetic energy with low entropy (few configurations, highly ordered) or low kinetic energy with high entropy (many configurations, disordered). The variables are independent.
\end{remark}

\section{The Snapshot Principle}
\label{sec:snapshot}
%==============================================================================

The velocity-entropy independence can be visualized through configurational snapshots.

\begin{definition}[Configurational Snapshot]
\label{def:snapshot}
A configurational snapshot is the spatial arrangement at instant $t$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}(t) = \{\mathbf{r}_1(t), \mathbf{r}_2(t), \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N(t)\}
\end{equation}
The snapshot records positions only---no velocities, kinetic energies, or temperature.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Snapshot Velocity Blindness]
\label{thm:snapshot_blind}
A configurational snapshot is compatible with any velocity distribution. For any snapshot $\mathcal{S}$ and any velocity distribution $\{\mathbf{v}_i\}$ satisfying conservation laws, there exists a physical state with positions $\mathcal{S}$ and velocities $\{\mathbf{v}_i\}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The snapshot $\mathcal{S} = \{\mathbf{r}_i\}$ contains no information about velocities. Positions and velocities are independent variables in phase space. For any set of positions, infinitely many velocity distributions are physically realizable (subject only to momentum and energy conservation).

The same spatial configuration can exist with arbitrarily low velocities (low temperature) or arbitrarily high velocities (high temperature). The snapshot is compatible with $T \in (0, \infty)$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Temperature-Snapshot Independence]
\label{cor:temperature_snapshot}
A given snapshot can exist at any temperature:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S} \text{ is compatible with } T \in (0, \infty)
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{example}[Crystal at Different Temperatures]
Consider a crystalline solid with molecules at lattice positions $\mathbf{r}_i = \mathbf{R}_i + \mathbf{u}_i$. At low temperature, velocities are small and vibrations minimal. At high temperature, velocities are large and vibrations vigorous. The snapshot---the lattice structure---is the same at both temperatures. The entropy (determined by accessible lattice configurations) is approximately the same, even though temperature differs by orders of magnitude.
\end{example}

The snapshot principle reveals why Maxwell's Demon fails: the demon observes velocities to sort molecules, but entropy depends on spatial arrangements. The demon is measuring the wrong quantity.

\section{Elastic Collisions: Temperature Without Entropy}
\label{sec:elastic}
%==============================================================================

If velocity and entropy are independent, processes that change velocity without changing spatial configuration should not change entropy. Elastic collisions confirm this.

\begin{theorem}[Elastic Collision Entropy Invariance]
\label{thm:elastic_entropy}
Elastic collisions change velocity distribution (and therefore temperature) without changing entropy.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider an elastic collision between molecules $i$ and $j$. Before collision: positions $\mathbf{r}_i(t^-)$, $\mathbf{r}_j(t^-)$; velocities $\mathbf{v}_i(t^-)$, $\mathbf{v}_j(t^-)$. After collision: positions $\mathbf{r}_i(t^+) \approx \mathbf{r}_i(t^-)$, $\mathbf{r}_j(t^+) \approx \mathbf{r}_j(t^-)$ (collision duration negligible); velocities $\mathbf{v}_i(t^+) \neq \mathbf{v}_i(t^-)$, $\mathbf{v}_j(t^+) \neq \mathbf{v}_j(t^-)$.

Energy is conserved: $E_i + E_j = E_i' + E_j'$. But kinetic energy is redistributed between molecules. If collisions are asymmetric, local temperature can change.

The spatial configuration is unchanged---all molecules have (approximately) the same positions before and after. The number of accessible arrangements $\Omega$ is unchanged. Therefore:
\begin{equation}
S(t^+) = \kB \ln \Omega(t^+) = \kB \ln \Omega(t^-) = S(t^-)
\end{equation}

Temperature can change while entropy remains constant. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Elastic collisions are isentropic (constant entropy) because they are reversible. This is consistent with the Second Law: reversible processes do not increase entropy. The point is that \textit{temperature} can change in reversible processes while entropy stays constant, demonstrating their independence.
\end{remark}

\section{The Category Error}
\label{sec:category_error}
%==============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Category Error]
\label{def:category_error}
A category error is the mistake of treating a property of one ontological category as if it were a property of another, or assuming causal relationships between independent categories. Let $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ be ontological categories. A category error occurs when one assumes:
\begin{equation}
\text{Manipulate property in } \mathcal{A} \Rightarrow \text{Change property in } \mathcal{B}
\end{equation}
when properties in $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ are independent.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Demon's Category Error]
\label{thm:demon_category}
Maxwell's Demon commits a category error: it treats velocity (kinetic category $\mathcal{K}$) as if it determined entropy (configurational category $\mathcal{C}$).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define the categories:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{K} &= \{\text{kinetic: } \mathbf{v}, E_{\text{kin}}, T, \mathbf{p}\} \\
\mathcal{C} &= \{\text{configurational: } \mathbf{r}, \Omega, S\}
\end{align}

The demon's strategy assumes:
\begin{equation}
\text{Sort by } v \in \mathcal{K} \Rightarrow \text{Change } S \in \mathcal{C}
\end{equation}

From Theorem~\ref{thm:velocity_arrangement}: $\partial S/\partial v = 0$, so $v \perp S$. The categories are orthogonal. The demon's assumption is false.

The category error is analogous to:
\begin{itemize}
\item Treating mental properties as physical (mind-body error)
\item Treating numbers as spatial objects (abstract-concrete error)
\item Treating temporal properties as spatial (time-space error)
\end{itemize}

In each case, the error conflates independent categories. The demon conflates kinetic and configurational categories, assuming manipulation of one affects the other. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Complete Strategy Failure]
\label{thm:complete_failure}
Every step of the demon's strategy is orthogonal to entropy.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The demon's operation decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
\text{Measure } v \to \text{Sort by } v \to \text{Change } T \to \text{(hope to change) } S
\end{equation}

Each arrow is broken:

\textbf{Step 1} (Measure $v$ $\to$ determine $T$): From velocity-temperature non-correspondence, the same velocity $v$ is ``hot'' in one ensemble and ``cold'' in another. Measuring velocity does not determine temperature contribution.

\textbf{Step 2} (Sort by $v$ $\to$ rearrange configurations): Sorting by velocity rearranges which molecule has which velocity, not which molecule is at which position. Velocity is the derivative of position, not position itself.

\textbf{Step 3} (Change $T$ $\to$ change $S$): From Theorem~\ref{thm:elastic_entropy}, temperature can change without entropy changing. These are independent quantities.

\textbf{Step 4} (hope to change $S$): From Corollary~\ref{cor:entropy_independent}, $\partial S/\partial v = 0$. Entropy depends on arrangements, not velocities.

The demon's strategy is orthogonal to entropy at every level. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Category Error vs. Information Error]
The category error resolution is distinct from information-theoretic resolutions. Information arguments assume the demon's strategy is correct in principle but fails due to measurement/erasure costs. The category error shows the strategy is incorrect in principle: sorting by velocity cannot change entropy even with free information, because velocity and entropy are in different categories.

This is more fundamental. Even resolving all information-theoretic objections (infinite memory, zero erasure cost), the demon still fails due to the category error.
\end{remark}

\section{What Actually Changes Entropy}
\label{sec:what_changes}
%==============================================================================

Having established what does not change entropy, we clarify what does.

\begin{proposition}[Entropy-Changing Operations]
\label{prop:entropy_changing}
Operations that change entropy are those that change spatial arrangements:

\textbf{(1) Mixing}: Bringing separated molecules into contact creates new configurations. $\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = \kB \ln(\Omega_{\text{mixed}}/\Omega_{\text{separated}}) > 0$.

\textbf{(2) Expansion}: Allowing access to new spatial regions. $\Delta S = N\kB \ln(V_f/V_i) > 0$ for $V_f > V_i$.

\textbf{(3) Chemical reaction}: Creating new species changes configuration space structure.

\textbf{(4) Phase transition}: Reorganizing spatial structure (solid $\to$ liquid increases accessible configurations).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Non-Entropy-Changing Operations]
\label{prop:non_changing}
Operations that do \textit{not} change entropy (in isolation) are those that change only kinetic properties:

\textbf{(1) Elastic collisions}: Redistribute velocity without changing positions. $\Delta S = 0$.

\textbf{(2) Velocity sorting}: Rearranges which molecule has which velocity, not positions.

\textbf{(3) Adiabatic compression/expansion}: Changes temperature via work, reversibly. $\Delta S = 0$.
\end{proposition}

The demon performs velocity sorting---a non-entropy-changing operation. The demon's strategy is doubly flawed: it performs an operation that cannot change entropy, and when entropy does change (through other mechanisms), it increases.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Comparison with Information-Theoretic Resolutions}

The Landauer-Bennett resolution correctly identifies that measurement and erasure have entropy costs. Our resolution does not contradict this but shows it is unnecessary for the core paradox.

\begin{table}[h]

The category error resolution is more fundamental: it shows the demon's strategy is misdirected regardless of implementation details.

\subsubsection{Are the Resolutions Compatible?}

Yes. The information-theoretic resolution is correct but incomplete. Measurement and erasure do have entropy costs. But these costs are not why the demon fails. The demon fails because velocity-sorting cannot change entropy, even if measurement and erasure were free.

Consider the hierarchy:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Category error} (most fundamental): Sorting by velocity $\perp$ changing entropy
\item \textbf{Information costs} (secondary): Even if strategy were sound, implementation costs would foil it
\item \textbf{Practical barriers} (least fundamental): Engineering challenges in building the demon
\end{enumerate}

Information-theoretic resolutions address level 2. We address level 1.

\subsection{Why Was the Category Error Overlooked?}

The conflation of kinetic and configurational properties is natural in classical thermodynamics because:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Temperature is traditionally defined through kinetic theory: $T \propto \langle v^2 \rangle$
\item Heat and entropy often change together in practice
\item The Clausius definition $dS = \delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$ links entropy to heat
\end{enumerate}

But correlation is not identity. Heat and entropy are \textit{correlated} in many processes because changing configurations often involves energy transfer. But they are not the \textit{same} quantity:
\begin{itemize}
\item Heat is energy in transit (kinetic)
\item Entropy counts arrangements (configurational)
\end{itemize}

The demon's thought experiment precisely separates these: the demon manipulates kinetic properties while hoping to change configurational properties. The separation reveals the category error.

\subsection{Implications for Thermodynamics}

The category error resolution has several implications:

\subsubsection{Entropy is Geometric}

Entropy counts spatial arrangements, not dynamic properties. This is more fundamental than the kinetic interpretation. The formula $S = \kB \ln \Omega$ is exact; the kinetic interpretation $dS = \delta Q/T$ is approximate (valid only for quasi-static processes).

\subsubsection{Heat-Entropy Decoupling is General}

The decoupling observed experimentally (Section~\ref{sec:experimental}) is not a special case but the general situation. Heat and entropy are independent quantities that happen to correlate in many circumstances.

\subsubsection{Information is Not Primary}

Information-theoretic arguments are correct but not necessary. The demon fails due to categorical structure, not information costs. This simplifies the resolution: no need to analyze memory, measurement, or erasure.

\subsection{Objections and Responses}

\subsubsection{Objection: Entropy Depends on Velocities in the Gibbs Formula}

The Gibbs entropy $S = -\kB \int f \ln f \, d^3r \, d^3v$ integrates over velocities. Does this mean entropy depends on velocities?

\textbf{Response}: The Gibbs formula integrates over velocity space but the distribution $f(v)$ at equilibrium is the Maxwell distribution, which is determined by temperature. The integral gives the \textit{same} result for any temperature: a constant times $N$. Entropy depends on the \textit{number} of particles, not their \textit{speeds}.

More precisely: the velocity integral contributes a multiplicative factor $\propto T^{3N/2}$ that is independent of spatial configuration. The spatial integral gives $V^N$. Together: $\Omega \propto V^N T^{3N/2}$, so $S \propto N\ln V + (3N/2)\ln T$. Temperature appears, but this is misleading---$T$ is not a property of individual velocities but a macrostate parameter. Sorting by velocity does not change $T$ (it changes velocity correlations while preserving distribution).

\subsubsection{Objection: Sorting Creates Temperature Difference}

The demon sorts molecules, creating hot and cold chambers. This is a temperature difference. Doesn't this affect entropy?

\textbf{Response}: Temperature difference is a kinetic property. Entropy depends on the number of accessible arrangements, which is unchanged by sorting. The chambers have different $T$ but the total $\Omega$ is unchanged.

Specifically: if chamber A has $N_A$ molecules and chamber B has $N_B$ molecules, the total arrangements are $\Omega = \Omega_A \times \Omega_B = V_A^{N_A} V_B^{N_B}$. This is unchanged by velocity sorting.

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

Maxwell's Demon fails not due to information costs but due to a category error. The demon manipulates velocity (kinetic category) to affect entropy (configurational category). Since $\partial S/\partial v = 0$, these categories are orthogonal. Manipulating one cannot affect the other.

\subsection{Summary of Results}

\textbf{Result 1: Velocity Independence of Entropy}
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial v_i} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{\partial S}{\partial v_i} = 0
\end{equation}
Entropy counts spatial arrangements. Velocity is the time derivative of position, not position itself. Changing velocity does not change arrangement count.

\textbf{Result 2: Snapshot Velocity Blindness}

The same spatial configuration can exist at any temperature:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S} = \{\mathbf{r}_i\} \text{ compatible with } T \in (0, \infty)
\end{equation}
Configurational snapshots contain no velocity information.

\textbf{Result 3: Elastic Collision Entropy Invariance}

Temperature can change without entropy changing:
\begin{equation}
S(t^+) = S(t^-) \quad \text{even when} \quad T(t^+) \neq T(t^-)
\end{equation}
demonstrating that temperature and entropy are independent quantities.

\textbf{Result 4: Category Error Theorem}

The demon's strategy conflates independent categories:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{K} &= \{\mathbf{v}, E_{\text{kin}}, T, \mathbf{p}\} \quad \text{(kinetic)} \\
\mathcal{C} &= \{\mathbf{r}, \Omega, S\} \quad \text{(configurational)}
\end{align}
These categories are orthogonal: $\mathcal{K} \perp \mathcal{C}$.

\textbf{Result 5: Experimental Validation}

Heat-entropy decoupling confirmed across 1066 tests:
\begin{itemize}
\item Heat fluctuates bidirectionally (49\% hot $\to$ cold, 51\% cold $\to$ hot)
\item Entropy is always positive ($\Delta S > 0$ in all tests)
\item Correlation $\rho(\Delta Q, \Delta S) = 0.003$ (no dependence)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Resolution in One Sentence}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{The demon commits a category error: sorting by velocity cannot change entropy because $\partial S/\partial v = 0$.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Final Statement}

The demon's strategy is fundamentally misdirected:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\text{Velocity: kinetic (rate of change of position)} \\
&\text{Entropy: configurational (count of arrangements)} \\
&\partial S/\partial v = 0 \text{ (orthogonal categories)} \\
&\text{Demon: category error}
\end{aligned}
}
\end{equation}

The demon does not exist because sorting by velocity cannot affect entropy. The Second Law protects configurational properties; the demon manipulates kinetic properties. The paradox dissolves.

This resolution is more fundamental than information-theoretic resolutions: the demon fails even with free measurement, free erasure, and infinite memory. The failure is conceptual, not practical.
